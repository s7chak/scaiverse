We live in a society, a true rainbow of every shade of human being.


I read through Wilber's Integral Theory to find a potential way to categorize all of knowledge that humankind has accumulated over eons. However, I come across the levels of thinking and got immediately drawn to the stages as per this framework. It's important to note that Ken Wilber's Integral Theory is a theoretical framework, an endeavor in build a categorical structure of the world we experience. Its applicability and validity is, of course, subject to ongoing debate and research within the fields of psychology, philosophy, and cognitive science. The framework referred as "All Quadrants All Levels" or AQAL Theory categorizes fields of knowledge into quadrants, stages of consciousness and awareness as levels of thinking, and lines representing progress through different intelligences and states and types and so on. And I think the all quadrant all levels naming comes from an attempt at attaining these levels, mastering all quadrants and levels leads to an integrated world - a version of an ideal world. This is not a scientific theory as Gravitational laws or thermodynamics, but I think it serves me one goal quite adeptly, and that is categorization.


About these levels, they go up from basal instincts and thinking patterns to more holistic, integrated, nuanced and possibly to a more conscious way of thinking. Going up is not necessarily good and staying down is not bad, or so they say. And that's it for the disclaimers before we go through them one by one. The levels are written from bottom to top as they make sense in altitudes of not really consciousness, but - it is difficult to put in words, so I'll use what I have for now - possibly sophistication.




  • Transcendent: Transcendent thinking represents the highest level of human consciousness, characterized by mystical experiences, spiritual awakening, and a sense of oneness with the universe. Individuals at this level may experience a profound sense of interconnectedness with all existence and a transcendence of the ego or individual self.

  • Universal: Universal thinking transcends individual perspectives and identities, embracing a sense of unity, compassion, and global responsibility. Individuals at this level develop a deep sense of interconnectedness with all beings and a commitment to the well-being of the entire planet.

    • ...
  • Pluralistic: Pluralistic thinking embraces diversity, inclusivity, and relativism. Individuals at this level recognize multiple perspectives and the importance of social justice and equality. They acknowledge that different cultures and worldviews can coexist and have validity within their respective contexts.

  • Rational: The rational level is characterized by logic, reason, and critical analysis. Individuals at this stage prioritize empirical evidence and scientific inquiry, relying on objective data and rational thought processes to understand the world around them.

  • Mythical: Mythical thinking involves adherence to traditional cultural narratives, religious dogma, and moral absolutes. Individuals at this level find a strong sense of community and belonging within the shared beliefs and rituals of their culture or religion.

  • Magical: At this level, individuals exhibit a magical or animistic worldview, where reality is interpreted through superstition, myth, and supernatural beliefs. Causality is often attributed to unseen forces or beings, and there is a lack of differentiation between the self and the external world.

  • Archaic: This is the most fundamental level, where individuals operate primarily on instinctual and survival-based behaviors. There is little to no self-awareness or cognitive complexity at this stage, as the focus is solely on meeting basic physiological needs.




These levels of thinking are neither rigid nor mutually exclusive. People generally exhibit characteristics of multiple levels simultaneously or glide between them fluidly sometimes with the same conversation. Also, the progression through these levels is not necessarily linear nor guaranteed. Some individuals may remain at lower levels due to various factors, such as cultural conditioning, personal experiences, or cognitive limitations.


It's important to recognize that each level of thinking serves a purpose and has value within its appropriate context. The lower levels, such as the archaic, magical, and mythical levels, provide a sense of belonging, community, and meaning, which are essential for psychological well-being and social cohesion. The higher ones, in this context, help see a bigger picture of humanity, reality and may bring their own flavors of wellbeing to the mind thinking at these stages.


The common wisdom about Integral Theory's levels of thinking is the following: While the transcendent and universal levels represent profound states of consciousness and awareness, they should not be pursued at the expense of neglecting the foundational levels that support our basic needs, practical functioning, and connection to our immediate communities and identities.

My understanding so far has this opinion/confusion: As we progress through the levels, and say one day we have a thought at the universal or eighth level, we do not lose our ability to think at lower levels. Even if some people find it amusing, intellectually orgasmic or life-affirmingly meaningful, the level implies the "host" is aware of all previous levels and recognizes their unique utility and contribution to their life.


The ability to navigate and integrate various levels of thinking is crucial for a balanced and holistic perspective, no doubt. Individuals who can appreciate and draw upon the strengths of different levels are better equipped to navigate the complexities of life and adapt to diverse situations. And so if my definition of the higher levels with the intrinsic awareness and acceptance of all that came before applies, we should be striving to think at the higher levels and keep returning back to reality, or pragmatism when the time calls.
